# Investment Philosophy

Date created: 2026-06-26

## Research objective

Develop an evidence-based investment philosophy for modern high-net-worth and ultra-high-net-worth wealth management clients. The core question is whether the industry shift toward passive investing means clients should simply track indexes, or whether the better model is a low-cost passive foundation combined with deliberate, proactive overlays such as tax management, tactical risk management, alternatives, direct indexing, and selective active exposure.

## Working thesis to test

The likely answer is not purely active or purely passive. The research should test whether the strongest current wealth management philosophy is:

> Broad, low-cost, tax-efficient passive or systematic core exposure, combined with high-conviction active decisions only where there is a durable edge, client-specific tax value, access advantage, liquidity premium, behavioral benefit, or planning need.

The research should separate evidence-supported practices from marketing narratives.

## Key research questions

1. Is passive index tracking still the highest-confidence default after fees, taxes, and implementation costs?
2. Where, if anywhere, does active management add value for taxable wealthy clients?
3. Does tactical allocation or sector rotation reliably improve after-tax, risk-adjusted outcomes?
4. Is a core/satellite model, for example 90% passive and 10% active, a practical compromise or mostly a branding framework?
5. How much value comes from tax alpha, including tax-loss harvesting, asset location, direct indexing, charitable planning, and gain deferral?
6. When do alternatives improve portfolios, and when do fees, illiquidity, opacity, and manager dispersion overwhelm the benefits?
7. Are covered calls and option overlays useful risk-management tools, income tools, or mostly return trade-offs?
8. How should investment philosophy differ for $2-20 million clients versus $20 million+ clients?
9. How should business owners, concentrated-stock holders, and clients with major liquidity events be handled differently?
10. What are leading RIAs, private banks, multifamily offices, and wealth management teams actually implementing today?

## Investment approaches to investigate

### Passive and indexed investing

- Market-cap-weighted index ETFs and mutual funds
- Broad global equity and fixed-income exposure
- Low-cost implementation
- Rebalancing discipline
- Evidence on active manager underperformance after fees
- Active share, closet indexing, and manager selection limits

### Passive core with proactive overlays

- Direct indexing
- Tax-loss harvesting
- Tax-aware rebalancing
- Asset location across taxable, tax-deferred, and tax-free accounts
- Transition management for embedded gains
- Custom restrictions and values-based exclusions
- Concentrated-stock diversification plans

### Tactical or active top-only overlays

- Sector rotation
- Factor tilts
- Risk-on/risk-off allocation
- Trend following and momentum overlays
- Valuation-aware rebalancing
- Macro tilts
- Manager selection in inefficient markets

### Core/satellite portfolios

Potential model to test:

- 80-95% strategic passive or systematic core
- 5-20% active satellite allocations where the investor may have a stronger case for edge, access, or diversification
- Satellite areas could include private credit, private equity, real assets, hedge funds, venture capital, thematic equity, or opportunistic tactical allocations

### Options and income overlays

Examples to evaluate:

- Covered calls
- Buy-write strategies
- Buffered ETFs
- Collars for concentrated positions
- Put-spread collars
- Option strategies as risk transfer versus alpha generation

A specific question to test: Is writing calls in a down market actually prudent, or does it cap recovery upside at the wrong time?

### Alternatives

Areas to research:

- Private equity
- Private credit
- Real estate
- Infrastructure
- Hedge funds
- Venture capital
- Interval funds
- Tender-offer funds
- Evergreen private-market funds
- Semi-liquid alternatives

Key evaluation criteria:

- Net returns after fees
- Dispersion by manager
- Access quality
- Illiquidity premium
- Valuation smoothing
- Leverage risk
- Tax complexity
- Suitability by client size

## Client segmentation framework

### $2-20 million clients

Likely needs to evaluate:

- Tax-efficient ETF/index portfolio as default foundation
- Direct indexing when account size and tax situation justify it
- Tax-loss harvesting and asset location
- Moderate alternatives only where suitability, liquidity, and access are strong
- Limited use of private funds due to minimums, complexity, tax reporting, and concentration risk
- Planning integration: retirement cash flow, estate planning, education, insurance, charitable giving, concentrated stock, and business sale proceeds
- Behavioral coaching and disciplined rebalancing as core value drivers

### $20 million+ clients

Likely needs to evaluate:

- Institutional-style asset allocation
- Custom direct indexing and transition management
- Greater access to private equity, private credit, hedge funds, venture, real estate, and co-investments
- Multi-entity household balance-sheet management
- Estate, GST, GRAT, IDGT, SLAT, charitable, and family-governance structures
- Liquidity tiering: operating cash, lifestyle reserves, strategic portfolio, illiquid capital
- Concentrated-stock hedging, collars, exchange funds, prepaid variable forwards, and charitable solutions
- Family-office or multifamily-office reporting and consolidated risk management
- Manager due diligence and access as a larger part of the value proposition

### Business owners

Special considerations:

- Business is often the largest and riskiest asset on the balance sheet
- Need to separate operating liquidity from personal wealth
- Pre-sale planning can be more valuable than post-sale investment selection
- Portfolio should account for business-sector exposure, leverage, cyclicality, and exit-timing risk
- Tax planning, estate planning, and charitable planning may dominate investment-product selection
- Post-liquidity-event portfolios may need staged diversification and psychological transition from entrepreneur to steward of liquid wealth

## Evidence standards

The final research should prioritize:

1. Peer-reviewed academic papers
2. Replicable empirical studies
3. Long-horizon evidence across market cycles
4. After-fee and after-tax outcomes
5. Risk-adjusted performance, not just gross return
6. Implementation feasibility for taxable wealthy clients
7. Evidence on investor behavior and real-world adherence
8. Transparency around conflicts, product incentives, and survivorship bias

## Preliminary hypothesis map

| Area | Likely evidence-backed role | Main risk or caveat |
|---|---|---|
| Passive index exposure | Default portfolio core | Not personalized for taxes, cash flows, or concentrated risks |
| Active equity management | Selective satellite only | Low persistence, high fees, taxable turnover |
| Factor investing | Possible systematic tilt | Cyclicality, crowding, tracking-error tolerance |
| Tactical allocation | Limited and disciplined use | Market timing failure and tax costs |
| Sector rotation | Needs strong evidence before use | High turnover and weak persistence |
| Direct indexing | Strong for taxable HNW clients | Complexity, tracking error, wash-sale management |
| Tax-loss harvesting | Potential source of tax alpha | Depends on volatility, tax rates, cash flows, and future gains |
| Covered calls | Income/risk-shaping tool | Caps upside and may underperform in rebounds |
| Alternatives | Useful for qualified clients with access | Fees, illiquidity, dispersion, opacity |
| Private credit | Income and diversification potential | Credit cycle, leverage, liquidity mismatch |
| Private equity | Potential illiquidity premium/access value | Manager selection and fee drag |
| Core/satellite | Practical governance framework | Can become product shelf marketing |
| Behavioral coaching | Often underappreciated value driver | Hard to quantify, requires advisor discipline |

## Bottom-line question

For wealthy taxable clients, the question is not simply active versus passive. The better question is:

> What combination of low-cost beta, systematic exposures, tax alpha, planning integration, behavioral discipline, and selective active/private opportunities produces the best after-tax, after-fee, risk-adjusted outcome for this specific household balance sheet?
